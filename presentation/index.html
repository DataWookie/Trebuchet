Trebuchet

A medieval siege weapon using a massive counterweight to hurl a projectile great distances.

The range of the Trebuchet was important so that the attacking army could stay outside the range of the defending archers' bows. The Trebuchet was invented in China around 500 BC. The Trebuchet developed over a period of around 1000 years, during which time it evolved significantly as a result of experimentation and innovation. The predecessors of the Trebuchet were the catapult and the ballista, both of which relied on an elastic mechanism to propel a projectile. As a result they were limited in the mass of the projectile (so they were really only suitable for human targets) as well as the accuracy which they could achieve.

Siege of Caffa (1346)
Biological Warfare at the 1346 Siege of Caffa, Historical Review, 8(9), 2002.

In the days before canons, the Trebuchet was used to hurl rocks large distances to break down stronghold walls. It has been called the Atomic Bomb of the Middle Ages. Howeveer, I think that it's actually more like the Ricin of the Dark Ages. The Crimean city of Caffa was heavily fortified by two concentric walls. The inner wall enclosed 6 000 houses, while the outer wall enclosed 11 000 houses. Caffa was besieged by invading Mongol forces in 1346 (it was under seige from the Mongols a number of times prior to this as well). The city was under siege for almost 3 years. During this time the Mongol army became afflicted with The Plague. The attackers hurled the plague cadavers into the city using Catapults and Trebuchets. The objective was two-fold:
  1. to deal with the Mongols' mortuary problem (literally "mountains of dead");
  2. in the hope that the "intolerable stench" would kill the inhabitants of Caffa.
The cadavers would have been badly mangled in the process. Many of them would have exploded on impact, spreading the disease. In many ways, Caffa is was the site of one of the most spectacular incidents of biological warfare ever. The Black Death, which swept through Europe between 1346 and 1353, claimed claimed between a quarter and a third of the population. The Mongol army hurled plague-infected cadavers into the besieged city. The disease was transmitted to the inhabitants of the city, some of whom carried it elsewhere in the Mediterranean Basin, particularly to Italy. This is the one of the first recorded cases of biological warfare.
Caffa is now known as Feodosiya. It's a city on the shore of the Black Sea. It was only besieged from the land, so it could still receive supplies from sea. Also, people could escape from the city to the sea.
The principle of the Trebuchet is very similar that to of a lever. You have a bar which pivots around a fulcum. A force is applied to one end of the lever (by the counterweight) and this transfer a force to the projectile and the other end of the lever. The power at each end of the lever must be the same. Since the velocity increases the further one gets from the fulcrum, so then the force must decrease. Ideally the long arm would be roughly four times as long as the short arm; the counterweight would be roughly 100 times as massive as the projectile. You'd want to fling a projectile of mass around 100 kg over a distance of at least 300 m (enough to be out of range of enemy archers). These were big machines with a massive counterweight. They were, as a result, slow to operate and could only fire around twice in an hour.

All of the counterweight's potential energy is converted into kinetic energy of the projectile. What is the optimal launch angle? What is the optimal range?

$$ V \mapsto T $$

Trebuchet converts all potential energy into kinetic energy.

So it is not dissimilar to firing a bullet from a rifle, the acceleration of a Ferrari, a catapult or the propulsion of a steam locomotive. Of course, none of those are exact analogues because they all convert chemical or elastic potential energy into kinetic energy. But the underlying principle is still the same.
$$ R = 2 \frac{M}{m} H $$
Interesting thing to note is that this is independent of the acceleration due to gravity. So a particular Trebuchet would have the same range on the Moon as it does on Earth despite the gravity being 1/6 weaker. 

immutable BlackBoxTrebuchet <: Trebuchet.TrebuchetBasic
    M::Float64                      # Counterweight mass
    m::Float64                      # Projectile mass
    H::Float64                      # Counterweight height
    efficiency::Float64             # Energy efficiency
    BlackBoxTrebuchet(M, m, H, e = 1) = new(M, m, H, e)
end

function maximum_range(T::BlackBoxTrebuchet)
    2 * T.M / T.m * T.H * T.efficiency
end

Seesaw Model

Lagrangian Mechanics

$$ \frac{d}{dt} \frac{\partial L}{\partial \dot q} - \frac{\partial L}{\partial q} = 0 $$

where $L = T - V$ is the Lagrangian.

$$ \ddot\theta = g \frac{M L_1 - m L_2}{M L_1^2 + m L_2^2} \sin\theta $$

which looks an awful lot like $ \ddot\theta \propto \theta $.

This is essentially the pendulum equation. In the small amplitude limit it is approximated by the equation for a harmonic oscillator.
$$ M L_1 - m L_2 > 0 $$

ensures that torque of counterweight is large enough.


immutable SeeSawTrebuchet <: Trebuchet.TrebuchetBasic
    M::Float64                      # Counterweight mass
    m::Float64                      # Projectile mass
    L1::Float64                     # Length of counterweight arm
    L2::Float64                     # Length of projectile arm
    L3::Float64                     # Length of the projectile sling
    L4::Float64                     # Length of the counterweight sling
    h::Float64                      # Height of pivot
    SeeSawTrebuchet(M, m, L1, L2, h) = new(M, m, L1, L2, 0, 0, h)
end
                        

function solve(T::SeeSawTrebuchet)
    function deriv(t, y)
        (y[1] < pi) ?
        [
            y[2];
            GravAccel * (T.M * T.L1 - T.m * T.L2) /
                        (T.M * T.L1^2 + T.m * T.L2^2) * sin(y[1])
        ] : [0.; 0.]
    end

    initial = [Trebuchet.initial_theta(T), 0];

    T, theta = ode23(deriv, initial, [0.; 5]);

    result = convert(DataFrame, hcat(T, hcat(theta...).'));
    names!(result, [symbol(i) for i in ["time", "theta", "thetadot"]])

    result[result[:theta] .< pi,:]
end
